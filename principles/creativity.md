# Creative Vision Principles in Photography

## Perspective and Point of View
**Perspective** is where you position the camera relative to the subject:
- Eye level: neutral, documentary.
- Low angle (looking up): subjects appear powerful, imposing.
- High angle (looking down): subjects appear smaller, vulnerable.
- Unusual angles (worm's-eye, bird's-eye, Dutch tilt): create disorientation or visual interest.

Shift perspective deliberately to change the viewer's relationship to the subject.

## The Decisive Moment
Coined by Henri Cartier-Bresson: the instant when visual elements align to create a meaningful, unrepeatable image. In street, documentary, and candid photography, anticipation and timing matter more than gear.

The decisive moment applies beyond photojournalism—any genre benefits from recognizing when fleeting elements (gesture, light, expression, juxtaposition) converge.

## Juxtaposition and Visual Relationships
Place contrasting or complementary elements together to create meaning:
- Old vs. new.
- Organic vs. geometric.
- Color contrasts (complementary colors on the color wheel).
- Scale contrasts (small subject in vast environment).

Juxtaposition invites the viewer to interpret the relationship, creating layers of meaning.

## Abstraction and Simplification
Abstract photography isolates patterns, textures, colors, or shapes from recognizable subjects. Simplification removes distracting elements, leaving only what serves the image's core idea.

Both approaches shift focus from "what is this?" to "how does this feel?" or "what does this suggest?"

## Color Theory
**Complementary colors** (opposite on the color wheel: red/green, blue/orange, yellow/purple) create vibrant contrast.
**Analogous colors** (adjacent on the wheel) create harmony.
**Monochromatic palettes** (variations of a single hue) evoke mood through tone and saturation, not hue contrast.

Color is emotional. Warm tones (reds, oranges, yellows) feel energetic or intimate. Cool tones (blues, greens) feel calm or distant. Use color intentionally to reinforce mood.

## Storytelling and Narrative
Every photograph implies a narrative, even if abstract. What happened before this moment? What might happen next? Who is this person, and what is their relationship to the environment?

Strong narratives emerge from:
- Capturing authentic moments.
- Providing context (environmental elements, secondary subjects).
- Sequencing images (photo essays, series).
- Ambiguity that invites interpretation.

## Rule-Breaking as Craft
Creativity often means deliberately violating conventional principles—but only after mastering them. Centered subjects, blown highlights, extreme grain, motion blur, lens flare—these "mistakes" become creative choices when used with intent.

Ask: "Does this choice serve the image's purpose?" If yes, break the rule.
