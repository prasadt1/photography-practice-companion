# Composition Principles in Photography

## Rule of Thirds
The rule of thirds divides the frame into a 3x3 grid. Place key subjects or points of interest along these lines or at their intersections to create balanced, engaging compositions. This technique prevents overly centered, static compositions and guides the viewer's eye naturally through the image.

## Leading Lines
Use natural or architectural lines (roads, rivers, fences, buildings) to draw the viewer's eye toward the main subject or through the scene. Leading lines create depth and direct attention purposefully.

## Framing
Use elements within the scene (doorways, windows, tree branches, arches) to create a natural frame around your subject. This focuses attention, adds depth, and provides context.

## Negative Space
Intentional use of empty or uncluttered space around the subject creates breathing room, emphasizes the subject, and can evoke emotion. Negative space is active, not wasteful—it contributes to the image's impact.

## Symmetry and Balance
Symmetrical compositions create harmony and formality. Asymmetrical balance (visual weight distributed unevenly but still feeling stable) creates tension and interest. Both are valid; choose based on the emotional intent of the image.

## Depth and Layering
Include foreground, midground, and background elements to create depth in two-dimensional photographs. Layering guides the eye through the scene and makes landscapes and environmental portraits more immersive.

## Breaking the Rules
Once you understand compositional principles, deliberately break them for creative effect. Central compositions can convey power; ignoring the rule of thirds can create tension. Intentional rule-breaking differs from compositional accidents.
