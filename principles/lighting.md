# Lighting Principles in Photography

## Golden Hour and Blue Hour
Golden hour (the hour after sunrise and before sunset) provides warm, soft, directional light ideal for portraits and landscapes. Blue hour (twilight) offers cool, diffused light. Both reduce harsh shadows and add atmospheric quality.

## Three-Point Lighting (Key, Fill, Rim)
**Key light** is the primary light source, establishing the scene's overall illumination and shadow direction.
**Fill light** softens shadows created by the key light without eliminating them entirely.
**Rim/Back light** separates the subject from the background by creating an edge highlight, adding depth and dimension.

This classical setup applies to portraits, product photography, and studio work.

## Hard Light vs. Soft Light
**Hard light** (small, direct sources like midday sun or bare flash) creates sharp, defined shadows and high contrast. Use for dramatic, sculptural effects.
**Soft light** (large, diffused sources like overcast skies or softboxes) wraps around the subject, creating gentle transitions and flattering skin tones. Use for even, approachable portraits.

## Color Temperature and White Balance
Light has color. Measured in Kelvin:
- Warm light (~2000-3500K): candlelight, incandescent bulbs, sunset.
- Neutral light (~5000-6500K): daylight, flash.
- Cool light (~7000-10000K): shade, overcast sky.

White balance corrects or creatively shifts color temperature. Consistent color temperature within a scene feels cohesive; mixed temperatures can appear accidental unless intentionally motivated.

## Direction of Light
**Front lighting**: even, flat, minimizes texture.
**Side lighting**: reveals texture, creates depth through shadows.
**Backlighting**: silhouettes or halo effects; requires fill or exposure compensation to retain subject detail.
**Top lighting**: can be unflattering in portraits (shadows under eyes/nose) but dramatic in architectural or landscape photography.

Direction shapes mood and dimensionality.

## Controlling Natural Light
Use reflectors to bounce light into shadows. Use diffusers (scrims, translucent fabric) to soften harsh sunlight. Position subjects relative to windows or open shade for controlled, directional natural light indoors.

## Exposure and Dynamic Range
Expose to preserve detail in highlights and shadows within your camera's dynamic range. Prioritize where detail matters most (e.g., skin tones in portraits, sky detail in landscapes). Use graduated neutral density filters or exposure bracketing for high-contrast scenes.
