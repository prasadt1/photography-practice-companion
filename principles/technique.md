# Technical Execution Principles in Photography

## Focus and Sharpness
**Critical focus** should land on the subject's most important feature (usually the eyes in portraits, the main subject in landscapes or still life). Use single-point autofocus for precision. Verify focus at 100% magnification in critical work.

**Sharpness throughout** requires adequate depth of field (controlled by aperture), fast enough shutter speed to prevent motion blur, and proper technique (stable grip, tripod when needed).

## Depth of Field
Controlled by **aperture** (f-stop), **focal length**, and **distance to subject**:
- Wide apertures (f/1.4–f/2.8) create shallow depth of field, isolating subjects from backgrounds. Ideal for portraits.
- Narrow apertures (f/8–f/16) extend depth of field, keeping more of the scene sharp. Ideal for landscapes.

Depth of field is a creative choice, not a default setting. Match it to your subject and intent.

## Shutter Speed and Motion
**Freeze motion** with fast shutter speeds (1/500s+). Essential for sports, wildlife, action.
**Blur motion intentionally** with slower shutter speeds. Panning (tracking a moving subject while using ~1/30–1/125s) keeps the subject sharp while blurring the background, conveying speed. Long exposures (seconds to minutes) smooth water, create light trails, capture star movement.

Motion blur is a tool, not a mistake—when used deliberately.

## ISO and Noise
ISO amplifies the sensor's sensitivity to light. Higher ISO enables shooting in low light but introduces noise (grain).
- Base ISO (typically 100-200): cleanest image quality.
- High ISO (1600+): noisier but necessary in dim conditions.

Modern cameras handle high ISO well. Noise is acceptable if it enables the shot; prioritize exposure and sharpness over noise paranoia.

## White Balance and Color Accuracy
Set white balance to match the light source for accurate color, or creatively adjust for mood (warming/cooling the image). Shoot RAW to adjust white balance non-destructively in post-processing.

Consistent white balance across a series creates visual cohesion.

## Histogram and Exposure
The histogram shows the distribution of tones from shadows (left) to highlights (right). Use it to avoid clipped highlights (pure white with no detail) or blocked shadows (pure black with no detail) unless intentional.

"Expose to the right" (ETTR): expose as bright as possible without clipping highlights, then recover shadow detail in post. Maximizes information captured.

## Image Stabilization and Support
Use in-body or lens-based image stabilization for handheld shooting. For shutter speeds slower than 1/(focal length) (e.g., 1/50s for a 50mm lens), stabilization or a tripod becomes critical to avoid camera shake blur.

Tripods, monopods, or stable surfaces enable intentional long exposures and ensure sharpness in low-light or high-resolution work.
